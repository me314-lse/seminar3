Part I: Probability Distributions

What is a probability distribution?

A probability distribution is a mathematical function that describes the likelihood of different outcomes for a random variable. In other words, it tells us how probable it is to observe particular values.

To make this more tangible, let’s consider a few real-life examples:

  • The height of university students

  • The grades received on a final exam

  • The number of heads in a series of coin tosses

Each of these can be modeled with a different type of probability distribution.

Before we can fully understand how data behaves in practice, we need two foundational ideas: the Law of Large Numbers and the Central Limit Theorem. Rather than starting with the definitions, let’s discover them with data first.

# simulate 30 additional rolls per row, drawn i.i.d. from a uniform distribution on 1:6
extra_rolls <- replicate(30, sample(1:6, nrow(dice_rolls), replace = TRUE))
colnames(extra_rolls) <- paste("Roll", 6:35)
rolls_extended <- cbind(dice_rolls, extra_rolls)

# row-wise means, now over all 35 rolls per sample
sample_means_35 <- rowMeans(rolls_extended, na.rm = TRUE)

ggplot(data.frame(sample_means_35), aes(x = sample_means_35)) +
  geom_histogram(
    aes(y = after_stat(density)),
    bins = 15,
    fill = "steelblue",
    color = "white"
  ) +
  stat_function(
    fun = dnorm,
    args = list(mean = mean(sample_means_35), sd = sd(sample_means_35)),
    color = "firebrick",
    linewidth = 1
  ) +
  labs(
    title = "Distribution of Sample Means (n = 35)",
    x = "Sample Mean",
    y = "Density"
  ) +
  theme_minimal()

The red curve is a theoretical normal distribution fitted to your sample means — notice how closely the histogram now tracks it. This is the Central Limit Theorem in action: with a large enough sample size, the sampling distribution of the mean becomes approximately normal, regardless of the shape of the original data (here, a uniform distribution over 1–6, which isn’t normal at all).

3. Exercise: Isolating what actually drives the Central Limit Theorem

Let’s discard the real dice data and simulate directly, so we can control two things separately: how many samples we take (n_sims) and how large each sample is (sample_size). This distinction is the single most common point of confusion with the CLT, so let’s make it visual.

set.seed(124)

sample_space <- 1:6

simulate_means <- function(pop, n_sims, sample_size = 30) {
  replicate(n_sims, mean(sample(pop, sample_size, replace = TRUE)))
}

The larger the sample size, the smaller the spread of the sample means: with more rolls per sample, each sample mean tends to fall closer to the population mean of 3.5. Increasing the number of simulations does not change this spread — it only makes the shape of the sampling distribution easier to see, since you’re drawing more sample means to plot.

Why this matters beyond dice

Think about a real opinion poll. Each poll is one “sample” of people, and the poll’s reported percentage is a sample mean (roughly).

This is exactly why estimates based on larger samples are more trustworthy: a poll or experiment with a large sample size produces an average that is likely to land close to the true population value — not because you ran it more times, but because each individual estimate is more stable.

Continuous Distributions

Gaussian Normal Distribution

The normal distribution is the most common distribution in our world. We can see it in heights, test scores, measurement errors. It’s symmetric and bell-shaped.

  • Key Parameters:
    • Mean: \(\mu\)
    • Variance: \(\sigma^2\)
  • Standard Normal Distribution has a \(\mu\) = 0 and a \(\sigma^2\)= 1.

Discrete Distributions

We’ll use one running example for the rest of this section: a basketball player who makes 70% of their free throws.

Bernoulli Distribution

A Bernoulli distribution has only two possible outcomes, namely 1 (success) and 0 (failure), and a single trial. It is thus a special case of the binomial distribution.

  • Parameter: p (probability of success)

Binomial Distribution

The binomial distribution models the outcome of a series of independent Bernoulli trials (coin flips), where each trial has two possible outcomes: heads (with probability \(p\)) or tails (with probability \(1-p\)).

  • \(n\) independent Bernoulli trials
  • Counts how many successes occur in those \(n\) trials
  • Parameters: \(n\) (number of trials), \(p\) (probability of success)

With only one game, you get a single number without a shape. With 1,000 games, a clear pattern emerges: most games cluster around 7 makes (consistent with \(p = 0.7 \times 10\) trials), with fewer games far above or below that. This is the same idea as the “1 test center vs. 1,000 test centers” comparison from a diagnostic test: a single trial-run tells you almost nothing about the underlying distribution; many repetitions reveal its actual shape.
